Luther Consulting SPRING 2015

A trip for our MAGIC mentors and kids from Harrison/West Lafayette/Jefferson

BoilerCode is mini hackathon designed for middle and high schools of both genders to come and explore the field of computer science in an interactive day of learning. The kids who came utilized the tool MIT App Inventor to create an app in 3 hours that they would present at the end of the hackathon. Anyone who is not familiar with the App Inventor will find it similar to a beginners programming language called Scratch, both using the concepts of drag and drop programming. There were roughly 25 kids who came to the hackathon, each kid was partnered with another participant and a mentor who would help them learn to use the App Inventor to create their very own app. Each mentor was required to go through a training session that would familiarize them with the programming language and brief them on what would be occurring during the hackathon. There was multiple meetings for the committee that was hosting the event, where the planning for the details of the hackathon happened. Where sub committees were made in order to organize who would be in charge of the food, forms, emails, activities, volunteers, schedules, mentors, training days and more.
The day of the hackathon, each kid received an official BoilerCode t-shirt as they checked in, as well as “SWAG” bag at the end of the day for completing their project. As the day began, they were gathered in a presentation room to hear what they would be doing for the day, and were introduced to each other. After each mentee found their mentor and when went to their directed lab to work in, where the mentor spend an hour and half teaching their kids how to use the app, and going over tutorials with them. Because their were so many mentors, it was easy to give as much attention needed to the kids, helping them understanding concepts like if statements, while loops, and how to connect things.










After the Introduction to Programming session, lunch broke out, where the kids were able to relax and talk with their mentors about opportunities in the field of Computer Science. Kids asked about what the mentors had programmed, what a real hackathon was like, and a lot of them were excited about future steps to learn more about how to program. After lunch, the kids got to begin working on their own mini coding project, where they brainstormed with their mentors for ideas. Then went on to explore how they were going to implement their ideas. Projects, like mini games, spin off versions of Flappy Bird, Cat Fact apps, popped up. Fortune cookie apps, and Phone Calling apps were all presented at the end. Kids learned how to work with sprites, and sensors in order while trying to create their app, along with learning the concepts of programming, using blocks to build bodies of code. Parents were invited in at the end of the day, to sit and hear all the presentations. Kids came up one by one with their apps downloaded to either a phone or kindle as they explained what their projects did. One kid explained their game that had an automated tank, that would try to hunt down another tank, in which you had to use the arrow buttons that were given to you, in order to maneuver out of the way, and shoot down the other tank. Another mentee came up and presented their app, where depending on your mood you would press on own of the colored blocks, and it which screen to a background with the mood on it and begin playing a sound clip that would fit your mood. The kids were very excited and proud of what they did at the end, and most if not all of them asked if the same event would be happening again next year.
